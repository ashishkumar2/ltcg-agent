INCOME TAX ACT, 1961 — SELECTED PROVISIONS FOR CAPITAL GAINS ON FOREIGN EQUITY
==============================================================================

SECTION 112A — TAX ON LONG-TERM CAPITAL GAINS ON SALE OF EQUITY SHARES

(1) Where the total income of an assessee includes any income chargeable under the
head "Capital gains" arising from the transfer of a long-term capital asset being
an equity share in a company or a unit of an equity oriented fund or a unit of a
business trust, the tax payable by the assessee on the total income shall be the
aggregate of—

(i) the amount of income-tax calculated on such long-term capital gains exceeding
one lakh rupees at the rate of ten per cent (raised to 12.5% from Finance Act 2024
with effect from 23rd July 2024); and

(ii) the amount of income-tax payable on the balance amount of the total income as
if such balance were the total income of the assessee.

(2) The provisions of sub-section (1) shall apply only if the transfer of the equity
share is chargeable to securities transaction tax.

NOTE: For foreign listed equities held by Indian tax residents, STT does not apply.
Instead, section 112A is applicable only to LTCG from equity shares listed in India.
For foreign equity (e.g. US stocks), general LTCG provisions apply.

HOLDING PERIOD FOR FOREIGN EQUITY:
Under the Finance (No. 2) Act 2024, unlisted securities (including foreign equity)
held for more than 24 months qualify as long-term capital assets.
Prior to the 2024 amendment, the threshold was 36 months for unlisted securities.

The Finance Act 2024 (effective 23 July 2024) reduced the holding period for foreign
equity from 36 months to 24 months.

SECTION 112 — TAX ON LONG-TERM CAPITAL GAINS (GENERAL)

(1) Where the total income of an assessee includes any income chargeable under the
head "Capital gains", arising from the transfer of a long-term capital asset (not
covered by section 112A), the tax payable by the assessee shall be—

(a) in the case of an individual or Hindu undivided family, twenty per cent of the
amount of such capital gains.

With effect from Finance Act 2024: LTCG on listed securities at 12.5% without
indexation benefit. LTCG on unlisted securities at 12.5% without indexation.

SECTION 111A — TAX ON SHORT-TERM CAPITAL GAINS

Where the total income of an assessee includes income chargeable under the head
"Capital gains" arising from transfer of a short-term capital asset being an equity
share in a company or a unit of an equity oriented fund—

(a) the income-tax payable shall be calculated at the rate of fifteen per cent
(raised to 20% from Finance Act 2024 with effect from 23rd July 2024).

For foreign equity (US stocks), short-term capital gains are taxed as per slab rate
or 20% as applicable under the Finance Act 2024.

GRANDFATHERING PROVISION (SECTION 112A PROVISO):

For long-term capital assets acquired before 1st February 2018, the cost of
acquisition shall be deemed to be the higher of—
(a) the actual cost of acquisition of such asset, and
(b) the lower of—
    (i) the fair market value of such asset as on 31st January 2018, and
    (ii) the full value of consideration received or accruing as a result of
         the transfer of such asset.

Fair Market Value on 31 January 2018:
- For listed equity: highest price quoted on a recognised stock exchange on 31 Jan 2018
- For unlisted equity (including foreign equity): book value method per Rule 11UA

NOTE: Grandfathering under section 112A strictly applies to assets chargeable to
STT (securities transaction tax). For foreign equity held by Indian residents, the
grandfathering benefit as codified in section 112A does not apply directly; however,
practitioners rely on the indexed cost of acquisition under section 48 for assets
acquired before the relevant indexation base year.

CBDT CIRCULAR ON EXCHANGE RATE FOR CAPITAL GAINS (FOREIGN ASSETS):

Per CBDT Circular No. 7/2023 and prior circulars, for computing capital gains on
transfer of a capital asset located outside India denominated in foreign currency:

- Cost of acquisition: convert to INR using the SBI TT Buying Rate (TTBR) prevailing
  on the date of acquisition.
- Sale consideration: convert to INR using the SBI TT Buying Rate (TTBR) prevailing
  on the date of transfer.

If the SBI TTBR is not available for the exact date (e.g. a bank holiday), use the
rate of the immediately preceding working day.

SCHEDULE FA — FOREIGN ASSETS DISCLOSURE:

Indian tax residents are required to disclose foreign assets under Schedule FA of
ITR-2 or ITR-3. This includes:

A. Details of Foreign Bank Accounts
B. Details of Financial Interest in any Entity outside India
C. Details of Immovable Property outside India
D. Details of any other Capital Asset held outside India
E. Details of accounts in which you have signing authority

For US equity holdings, each brokerage account and each holding must be separately
reported under Schedule FA Part B and Part D.

EXEMPTION LIMIT FOR LTCG (SECTION 112A):

With effect from Finance Act 2024 (Budget 2024-25):
- Exemption: Rs. 1.25 lakh per financial year (increased from Rs. 1 lakh)
- Rate above exemption: 12.5% (increased from 10%)
- Effective for transfers made on or after 23rd July 2024

CARRY FORWARD OF LOSSES:

Under section 74 of the Income Tax Act:
- Short-term capital losses can be set off against both short-term and long-term
  capital gains.
- Long-term capital losses can only be set off against long-term capital gains.
- Unabsorbed capital losses can be carried forward for 8 assessment years.

TAX LOSS HARVESTING:

Tax loss harvesting involves crystallising unrealised losses to offset capital gains.
Key considerations for Indian residents holding US equity:
1. Wash sale rules do not apply under Indian tax law (unlike US law).
2. Losses must be realised (disposed of) before the end of the financial year
   (31 March) to be usable in that assessment year.
3. Short-term losses are more flexible — they can offset both STCG and LTCG.
4. After harvesting, the investor can immediately repurchase the same securities
   without any wash sale restriction under Indian law.

FOREIGN TAX CREDIT (SECTION 90/91):

If US taxes (e.g. dividend withholding tax) are paid on foreign income, Indian
residents can claim foreign tax credit under section 90 (where DTAA exists) or
section 91 (unilateral relief). The India-US DTAA provides relief on double taxation.

Capital gains arising to an Indian resident from sale of US securities are generally
taxable only in India (the resident state) under Article 13 of the India-US DTAA,
unless the securities derive value principally from immovable property.
